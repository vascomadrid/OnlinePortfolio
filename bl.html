Becoming Latino

Introduction

>

Reading Latinx literature throughout this semester has taught me how migration affects one’s notion of temporality. Some migrants never stop moving. I want to trace how my movements have affected how I perceive binaries. I want to know how my fluidity in gender and age transcribe onto the shifts of my nation. I want to explore how others who do not move see my movement.

My mother never directly tells me that I hurt her. She would announce it such that it is a truth and no matter the context or the affect, that truth would be absolutely true. Such an announcement does not need response.
If she told me directly maybe I could.
She sneaks her messages into songs and dances: “A mother has little love for a son who did not give her pain”
In the states she is both my mother and mi abuelita.
I can’t help but romanticize her.

These past few years, at least more so than the few years prior, I have taken on the ambiguous life.
Subjectivity is a sword.
The middle is not the middle point, or a point at all.
Few things are more fundamental than time, space, and community. Each is their own dimension and if you are lucky you maintain a single combination of the three.

I tested my placement. Erasing stillness so that movement is the adjective of my space.
When I was walking alone I heard chirps of “Sir.” As I turned the chirps switched to “mam.” This was the first time I felt a multidimensional shift.
Though I had moved everyone else stayed in their place. Gender was left in the last dimension.
The parakeet asked me for my age, amazed, that someone who looks so young could be a college student.
In what temporal reality is it possible for me to perform without question?
The first parakeet could not understand that I did not have a gender and the second could not understand that if I could not have an age, I would most definitely opt out.
For most, mathematics is an absolute truth. But yet, when I tell them I am twenty, they don’t seem to understand.
“The still” fail to recognize that different parts of a pattern depend on one another and were one to shift, as it desires, the rest are bound to gain inertia.

The parakeet asked me if I am from Eastern Asia.
I tell him I am Latino.
I leave out my x.
In this realm those identities take up the same space like sugar and water.
I tell the parakeet that in other realms I exist outside of that space.
The parakeet practices rhetoric. Don’t binaries only have an interior space?

My mother is both my mother and mi abuelita.
Because family is important. And if your family is trapped within a border, you imagine them in others. Their forms are not unique.
My Latinidad sometimes get brought into question.
The questions take on sentience.
My mother knows my fate.
In the realms of others, my people live with gas masks on the streets.
They surround themselves with bombs.
My people do not know who they are.

We do not have access to ourselves in Venezuela.

Information is distributed intra-dimensionally. The displaced are outside of that space.
I am displaced. I never did think of myself as Venezuelan until I started to dislike the Eagle.
One time I moved from one realm to another.
Simon Bolivar walked me through my heritage.
He would laugh at me when I spoke Spanish because I cannot roll my Rs.
I told him that in some realms I am as real as my mother.

My mom also buys pizza every Thursday from Papa Johns.
She has a waffle maker but instead of making waffles it makes arepas.
It takes in balls of corn flour and returns
A Venezuela rid of un dictador!
I preferred the pizza to arepas.

Sometimes I do not know my gender the same way I do not know my nationality.
I wonder if I can project one onto the other.
Maybe there is a loop.
There is a point where one starts.
A point where it stops.
And a point where it begins again.

I move through my Latinidad the way my parents moved through South America.
I move through my gender the way my parents moved through Florida.

I wonder if a migrant can ever be still.

When I was four, my parents woke me up late into the night. I could not see and I did not want to. We listened to “La Vaca Mariposa” on the way to the airport. I dreamt the flight.

A migrant can never be still.

My mother feared my fate.
